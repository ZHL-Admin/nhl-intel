Every deadline someone points at a winger's skating data and calls the decline before it happens. The theory is intuitive, and that is exactly why it is dangerous: burst speed goes first, production follows a season later. It sounds too clean to be true, so we tested it against every skater with two consecutive qualified tracking seasons.

The setup is deliberately literal. For each player, take the year-over-year change in his 22+ mph burst rate — bursts per sixty minutes, straight from the league's on-ice tracking — and line it up against the change in his scoring the *following* season, measured in points per eighty-two games. If the legs really lead the points, the relationship should be obvious.

<PullStat value="r = 0.064" label={<>Correlation, year-over-year burst change vs.<br/>next-season scoring change · N = 1,588</>} />

It is not obvious. It is barely there. Across 1,588 paired player-seasons the correlation is 0.064 — statistically detectable at p = 0.010 because the sample is large, and practically useless because the effect is a rounding error. A player losing a step tells you almost nothing about next year's scoring that his age and role were not already telling you.

We set the bar for publishing a decline flag at a correlation of 0.10 before running the test, precisely so a large sample couldn't launder a trivial effect into a "signal." At 0.064 the test fails its own bar, and the flag stays out of the player pages. The clean theory does not survive contact with the data — which is the whole point of writing the negative result down instead of quietly shelving it.

That is not the same as saying skating doesn't matter. It says the *decline-predicts-collapse* shortcut doesn't, at least not at a horizon and effect size worth flagging. The legs and the points move together over a career; one does not reliably run a year ahead of the other.
