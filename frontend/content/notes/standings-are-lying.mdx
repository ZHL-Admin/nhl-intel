A standings table looks like a ranking, but it is really a single sample. The season happened once; the bounces fell the way they fell. Re-run it — same shot quality, same chances, different puck luck — and you get a different table. Do that ten thousand times, average the points, and you have what each team *deserved* as opposed to what it *banked*.

The gap between the two is luck, and it is not small.

Boston banked a hundred points on the strength of a season worth about seventy-seven — nearly twenty-three points of pure overperformance against its own chances. Montreal and Buffalo sit almost exactly as far ahead. At the other end, Vancouver's 58 points understate a team that deserved closer to 74; the Canucks left roughly sixteen points on the ice that a fairer season would have handed back.

None of this says Boston is secretly bad or Vancouver secretly good. It says the *points* are a noisy readout of the play, and the four clubs riding the biggest tailwinds are the ones whose next result you should trust least. When a team's record and its deserved record disagree by twenty points, the record is the thing that has to move.

That gap — results minus deserved — is the luck column on the [Power Ratings](/ratings) table, kept in view precisely so a hot start can't masquerade as a good team.
